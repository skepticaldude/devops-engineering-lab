# Managing Resistance to Automation in SRE

When organizations begin implementing **automation** to eliminate **toil**, some individuals may resist the change.

People react differently to automation initiatives. Some may feel that:

- Their jobs are at risk
- Certain tasks are not actually toil
- Automation is unnecessary

Because of this, leaders must understand **how to manage resistance to change** within their organization.

---

# The Psychology of Change

Change naturally triggers emotional reactions. Even when a change is beneficial, both **positive and negative responses** should be expected.

People generally fall into **four emotional categories** during organizational change.

---

# Four Types of Reactions to Change

## Navigators

Navigators are individuals who actively help move the organization forward.

Leaders should:

- Identify these individuals early
- Celebrate their behavior
- Empower them as **champions of change**

These individuals can help drive adoption across teams.

---

## Critics

Critics are passionate and energetic individuals who challenge the change.

Their concerns often come from **valid fears or strong engagement** with the organization.

Leaders should:

- Listen to their concerns
- Engage with them directly
- Address their fears

Once convinced, critics can become **powerful advocates for change**.

---

## Victims

Victims often take organizational changes **very personally**.

They may feel overwhelmed or emotionally impacted by the change.

Leaders should:

- Listen actively
- Show empathy
- Allow them to express their emotions

Once they feel heard, they are more likely to become receptive to the change.

---

## Bystanders

Bystanders are difficult to read.

They may appear indifferent or unaware that change is happening.

Leaders should:

- Communicate clearly with them
- Ask for their feedback
- Understand their perspective

Without engagement, bystanders may simply **continue working as if nothing has changed**.

---

# Emotional Reactions to Change

A single person may move between multiple emotional categories during a change process.

Most people experience these reactions at different points in their careers.

Leaders should remember:

> Teams often mirror the emotional responses of their leaders.

How leaders handle change strongly influences **how teams respond to it**.

---

# The Neuroscience of Change

Human brains are wired to react strongly to change. Several biological responses occur during transitions.

### Social Exclusion

Feeling excluded activates the **anterior cingulate cortex**, the same area of the brain associated with **physical pain**.

---

### Deception Anxiety

If people feel misled or discover unrealistic expectations, the **prefrontal cortex** triggers heightened anxiety and suspicion.

---

### Self-Solved Success

Solving problems independently releases **adrenaline**, creating a strong sense of accomplishment.

---

### Cognitive Overload

The prefrontal cortex can process only a limited number of ideas at once.

When people become overwhelmed by unfamiliar concepts, the **amygdala** activates, causing feelings such as:

- Anxiety
- Fear
- Anger
- Depression
- Fatigue

---

### Habit Formation

Tasks that are repeated frequently become **automatic habits**.

These require little mental effort, which is why people prefer familiar routines.

---

# How Leaders Can Manage Change Reactions

Organizations can reduce resistance by applying several practical strategies.

| Brain Response | Recommended Leadership Action |
|----------------|--------------------------------|
| Exclusion pain | Involve people in the change process |
| Deception anxiety | Set realistic expectations |
| Self-solve adrenaline | Encourage collaboration and coaching |
| Amygdala overload | Simplify communication |
| Attention density | Use engaging training and communication |
| Habit disruption | Allow time for new habits to develop |

---

# The Change Transition Curve

Change typically occurs in **three stages**:

1. **Beginning**
2. **Middle**
3. **End**

However, individuals often move **forward and backward** through these stages before fully adapting.

Leaders must support teams throughout the entire process.

---

# Presenting Change as an Opportunity

Change should be framed as an **opportunity rather than a threat**.

To communicate change effectively, leaders should connect with people on three levels.

---

# The Head, Heart, and Feet Framework

## Head (Rational)

Explain:

- Why the change is happening
- The strategic mission
- The long-term vision
- The business rationale

---

## Heart (Emotional)

Address the personal impact.

People want to understand:

- Why the change matters
- How it affects their daily work
- What benefits they gain from the change

Helping people feel **included in the transformation** increases engagement.

---

## Feet (Behavioral)

Provide the tools needed for success:

- Training
- Knowledge
- Skills
- Resources

Teams must feel **capable and supported** when adapting to change.

---

# Why Change Efforts Fail

Research shows that **resistance to change is the main reason organizational transformations fail**.

Most resistance comes from **fear of loss**, such as losing:

- Job security
- Control
- Competence
- Relationships
- Direction

Humans tend to react **more strongly to potential loss than to potential gain**.

---

# Checklist for Managing Change Successfully

Leaders should evaluate the following questions when introducing SRE practices:

- Are leaders role-modeling the new behaviors?
- Do people understand the reason for the change?
- Do people care about the change being successful?
- Do people have the knowledge and ability to succeed?
- Are reinforcement and recognition systems in place?

If any of these answers are **no**, leadership should address them immediately.

Successful transformation requires **all of these elements**.

---

# Real-World Example: Banking Industry

One major commercial bank in Spain attempted to automate several operational processes.

The **VP of Engineering**, who sponsored the SRE initiative, invested heavily in automation to demonstrate its business benefits.

However, engineering teams were **reluctant and skeptical**.

The main issue was **fear of job loss**.

Because leadership had not clearly explained:

- The goals of automation
- The impact on teams
- How roles would evolve

employees perceived automation as a **direct threat to their jobs**.

This lack of communication created strong resistance.

---

# Key Takeaway

Successful SRE adoption requires more than technical implementation.

Organizations must also:

- Understand the **psychology of change**
- Address emotional reactions
- Communicate clearly
- Support teams throughout the transition

Automation and SRE practices succeed only when **people are supported during the change process**.
